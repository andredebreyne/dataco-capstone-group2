# Exploratory Data Analysis

EDA was used as a validation and design-support step before modeling, not as a separate analytical objective. Its purpose was to check data quality, understand target distributions, inspect descriptive patterns in order-time and pre-dispatch fields, and identify feature-governance issues that could affect AO1, AO2, and AO3.

The EDA artifacts confirmed that the working Silver clone contained 180,519 rows and 53 columns. Data-quality review found that most reviewed variables had usable missingness profiles, but some fields required exclusion or deferral. `Order Zipcode` had high missingness, raw order timestamps and identifiers had high cardinality, and granular geography fields such as city, postal code, latitude, and longitude were not appropriate as direct first-pass model features. Commercial fields also showed outliers and duplication risk, so they required AO2 target-reconstruction review before modeling use.

For AO1, EDA confirmed that `Late_delivery_risk` was a valid binary target with no missing or invalid target rows in the Silver clone. The late class represented 98,977 rows, or 54.83%, while the not-late class represented 81,542 rows, or 45.17%. This is a mild imbalance rather than a rare-event problem, but it still supported reporting recall, precision, F1, confusion matrix, and AUC rather than relying on accuracy alone. AO1 bivariate EDA showed descriptive variation across planned service fields. For example, one scheduled shipping day and First Class shipping each had a 95.32% late-delivery rate, while four scheduled days and Standard Class shipping each had a 38.07% rate. These patterns supported retaining planned-service variables for leakage-screened Gold review, without treating them as causal effects or final feature-selection decisions.

For AO2, EDA confirmed `Order_Profit_Per_Order` as the target and showed that profitability was skewed and outlier-sensitive. The target had a mean of 21.97, median of 31.52, standard deviation of 104.43, minimum of -4,274.98, maximum of 911.80, skewness of -4.742, and 18,942 IQR outliers. Product mix also showed descriptive profitability variation, including department and category differences. These findings supported using error metrics such as RMSE and MAE and preserving the AO2 target-reconstruction caution around commercial predictors.

For AO3, EDA supported the need to combine risk and expected profitability rather than rely on one descriptive signal alone. Late-delivery risk varied most clearly across planned service fields, while profitability varied through product mix and commercial structure. EDA therefore functioned as a validation and modeling-design step that helped shape leakage control, chronological splitting, feature governance, and the later risk-margin prioritization framework.
